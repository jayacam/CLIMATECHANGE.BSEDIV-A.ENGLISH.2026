🌍 Meteorology & Climate Science

🌤️ Weather Elements

Weather: The state of the atmosphere at a particular time and place.

Element Definition Key Technical Terms
Temperature How hot or cold the air is; measures heat energy in the atmosphere —
Humidity Amount of water vapor in the air; warm air holds more than cold air Water vapor: Invisible gaseous form of water (H₂O) in the air
Cloudiness How much of the sky is covered by clouds; forms when humid air rises, cools, and condenses Condensation: Process where water vapor changes into liquid water droplets as air cools
Precipitation Water falling from sky—rain, snow, sleet, hail Sleet: Ice pellets formed when rain freezes before hitting ground; Hail: Balls of ice formed in strong updrafts of thunderstorms
Wind Air moving from high pressure to low pressure; driven by uneven solar heating and Earth's rotation High pressure: Region where air sinks, creating denser, heavier air; Low pressure: Region where air rises, creating lighter, less dense air
Pressure (Atmospheric Pressure) Weight of air above a point; high pressure usually brings clear weather, low pressure brings storms Atmospheric pressure: Force exerted by the weight of the atmosphere above a surface
💡 Connection: These elements don't exist in isolation. Temperature differences create pressure differences, which create wind. Wind moves moisture (humidity), which forms clouds, which produce precipitation. It's one interconnected system.

💨 Easterlies & ITCZ

Easterlies

Easterlies: Prevailing winds blowing from east to west (named by origin, not destination).

🌴 Tropical Easterlies (Trade Winds)

  • Blow from northeast (Northern Hemisphere) and southeast (Southern Hemisphere)
  • Located 0°–30° latitude
  • Steady, reliable, generally fair weather
  • Historically used by sailing ships for commerce
Trade winds: Consistent tropical winds historically used by sailing ships for commerce ("trade")
Latitude: Distance north or south of the equator, measured in degrees

❄️ Polar Easterlies

  • Blow from east in polar regions (60°–90° latitude)
  • Cold, dry, less consistent than tropical easterlies
  • Air flows outward from high pressure at poles toward lower pressure at ~60° latitude
Polar regions: Areas around Earth's North and South Poles, characterized by extreme cold

Intertropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ)

ITCZ: A band near the equator where northern and southern trade winds converge, forcing warm, moist air to rise, creating a zone of low pressure, towering clouds, heavy rainfall, and frequent thunderstorms.

Characteristic Description
Nickname "Rain belt of the tropics"
Movement Shifts north and south seasonally with the sun
Impact Influences wet or dry seasons and tropical cyclone formation
Key Terms Summary:
• Equator: Imaginary line at 0° latitude dividing Earth into Northern and Southern Hemispheres
• Convergence: Coming together of air masses or winds
• Rising air (updraft): Air moving upward; cools and forms clouds
• Tropical cyclone: Rotating storm system forming over tropical waters

🌧️ Monsoons of the Philippines

Amihan (Northeast Monsoon)

  • Duration: November to May
  • Origin: Siberia and China
  • Characteristics: Cool, dry winds
  • Translation: "North wind"
  • Weather: Cooler, pleasant, less rain
  • East coasts: Drizzle
  • Inland: Drier conditions (especially Christmas season)
Monsoon: Seasonal reversal of winds bringing distinct wet and dry periods
Drizzle: Very light, fine precipitation consisting of small water droplets
Siberia: Vast region in northern Asia known for extremely cold winter temperatures

Habagat (Southwest Monsoon)

  • Duration: June to October
  • Characteristics: Heavy rains, thunderstorms, humid weather
  • Dangers: Floods and landslides
  • Enhancement: Often worsened by typhoons
  • Opposite: Cooler, drier amihan
Thunderstorm: Storm with thunder, lightning, heavy rain, and sometimes hail or strong winds
Flood: Overflow of water onto normally dry land
Landslide: Movement of rock, earth, or debris down a slope
Typhoon: Powerful tropical cyclone in the western Pacific Ocean
Feature Amihan Habagat
Direction Northeast Southwest
Feel Cool, dry, pleasant Hot, humid, wet
Rain Light drizzle, mostly dry Heavy rain, storms
Problems Minimal Floods, landslides
Season November–May June–October

🌡️ Climate Fundamentals

What is Climate?

Climate: The long-term effect of solar radiation and rotating Earth's varied surface on the atmosphere. Understood most easily in terms of annual or seasonal averages of temperature and precipitation.

Key Terms Breakdown:
• Solar radiation: Energy emitted by the sun, traveling as electromagnetic waves
• Annual average: Mean value calculated over one full year
• Seasonal average: Mean value calculated over a specific season (e.g., summer, winter)

Greenhouse Effect

Natural process where greenhouse gases in the atmosphere trap heat from solar radiation.

Step Process Key Terms
1 Solar radiation enters the atmosphere —
2 Earth absorbs it and re-radiates it as infrared radiation (heat) Re-radiate: Release absorbed energy again; Infrared radiation: Heat energy emitted by warm objects
3 Gases like CO₂, methane, and water vapor absorb this heat CO₂: Carbon dioxide; Methane (CH₄): Potent greenhouse gas
4 They re-emit it in all directions—some escapes to space, some returns to Earth Re-emit: Release absorbed energy in all directions
5 Result: Planet stays warmer than it would be otherwise Essential for life, but human activities are enhancing it excessively
Without the greenhouse effect: Earth would be too cold for life.
Current problem: Human activities are enhancing (intensifying) it beyond natural levels.

🔥 Global Warming & Climate Change

Global Warming

Global warming: The long-term increase in Earth's average surface temperature. Primarily anthropogenic—caused by greenhouse gas emissions from burning fossil fuels, deforestation, and agriculture.

Key Metrics

Global mean surface temperature (GMST): Average temperature has risen ~1.1°C since pre-industrial times.

Important distinction: A cold day doesn't disprove it. It's about the trend over decades.

Anthropogenic: Caused by human activities
Fossil fuels: Energy sources formed from ancient organic matter (coal, oil, natural gas)
Deforestation: Clearing or thinning of forests by human activity
Pre-industrial times: Period before ~1850, used as climate baseline
Trend: Long-term pattern or direction of change over time

Climate Change

Climate change: Global warming plus all its downstream effects:

System Effect Technical Terms Defined
Cryosphere Glacial retreat, permafrost thaw, polar ice loss Cryosphere: All frozen parts of Earth's surface; Glacial retreat: Shrinking of glaciers; Permafrost: Permanently frozen ground
Hydrological cycle Water scarcity, intensified precipitation, droughts Hydrological cycle: Continuous movement of water in Earth system; Precipitation: Any water falling from atmosphere
Biosphere Range shifts, phenology changes, coral bleaching Biosphere: All living organisms on Earth; Phenology: Timing of seasonal biological events
Human systems Agricultural disruption, heat stress, climate migration, economic costs Agricultural: Related to farming; Heat stress: Health harm from excessive heat
Extreme events Stronger hurricanes, heatwaves, wildfires Hurricanes: Large tropical storms with strong winds; Heatwaves: Prolonged excessive heat

Scientific Basis

Evidence What It Shows Technical Terms
Ice cores Ancient atmospheric composition, past temperature Ice cores: Cylinders of ice drilled from glaciers containing trapped air bubbles from thousands of years ago
Satellite data Radiative forcing, ice melt, sea level Radiative forcing: Difference between incoming solar energy and outgoing infrared energy
Climate models Projections match observations when human emissions included Projections: Estimates of future conditions based on current data and mathematical models
Paleoclimate data Past climate sensitivity to CO₂ Paleoclimate: Climate of the distant past; Climate sensitivity: Warming expected from doubling of atmospheric CO₂

Scientific Consensus

IPCC (Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change): International body assessing climate science. Thousands of scientists agree the signal is clear and attributable to human activity.

🌱 Solutions & Actions

Reducing Carbon Footprint

Sector Actions Technical Terms Defined
Energy Switch to renewables (solar, wind), improve efficiency, electrify transport Renewables: Energy sources naturally replenished; Efficiency: Using less energy for same service; Electrification: Converting to electricity use
Transportation Active transport (walk, bike), public transit, EVs, reduce air travel Active transport: Human-powered movement; EVs: Electric vehicles; Public transit: Shared transportation systems
Consumption Reduce, reuse, recycle, buy local, minimize waste Local: Produced nearby, reducing transportation emissions
Diet Reduce meat (especially ruminant—high methane) Ruminant: Hoofed animals (cattle, sheep) that produce methane through digestion
Land use Reforestation, afforestation, protect carbon sinks Reforestation: Replanting trees in previously forested areas; Afforestation: Creating new forests; Carbon sinks: Natural reservoirs that absorb more carbon than they release
Policy Support carbon pricing, regulations, Paris Agreement Carbon pricing: Economic cost assigned to carbon emissions (taxes or cap-and-trade); Paris Agreement: 2015 international treaty on climate change

Adaptation Strategies

🏗️ Resilient Infrastructure

Building systems that can withstand and recover from climate shocks.

Resilient: Able to withstand and recover from shocks and stresses

⚠️ Early Warning Systems

Networks monitoring hazards and alerting populations before disasters strike.

🌾 Sustainable Agriculture

Farming meeting current needs without compromising future generations' ability to meet theirs.

💧 Water Management

Planning, developing, distributing, and managing water resources.

Why Every Action Matters

Every ton of CO₂ not emitted helps. Greenhouse gases are cumulative—every reduction delays and reduces peak warming.

We have a limited carbon budget to stay below 1.5°C or 2°C. Every choice affects that budget.

Carbon budget: Total amount of CO₂ emissions allowed to keep warming below specific targets
1.5°C / 2°C: Temperature targets set by Paris Agreement to limit dangerous climate impacts
Cumulative: Building up gradually over time; greenhouse gases accumulate in the atmosphere
